ICC-ES Reports (ESRs) in Missouri: What They Are and How a Professional Engineer Can Help

March 30, 2026

Many construction products, including anchors, connectors, proprietary structural systems, specialty fasteners, and building materials, are supported by an ICC-ES Evaluation Report (ESR). In Missouri, ESRs are commonly used during design, permitting, and inspection to demonstrate that a product meets applicable code requirements.

This article explains what an ICC-ES report is, how it's used in Missouri, and when involvement from a Professional Engineer (PE) may be helpful.

What Is an ICC-ESR?

An ICC Evaluation Service Report (ICC-ESR) is a third-party document that evaluates a product for compliance with model building codes such as the International Building Code (IBC) or International Residential Code (IRC).

An ESR typically includes:

  • Product description and intended uses
  • Applicable code references
  • Design values and limitations
  • Installation requirements
  • Conditions of use

Local jurisdictions often accept ESRs as evidence that a product meets code intent when used as specified.

How ESRs Are Used in Missouri

Missouri jurisdictions such as St. Louis, Kansas City, Columbia, Springfield, and surrounding municipalities generally adopt versions of the IBC/IRC and reference ICC-ES reports during plan review.

Common uses include:

  • Permit submittals: Demonstrating that a proprietary product complies with code
  • Plan review: Verifying design values such as anchor capacities and allowable loads
  • Inspections: Confirming installation matches ESR conditions
  • Substitutions: Supporting alternate materials or systems

While ESRs are widely accepted, local authorities having jurisdiction ultimately determine whether a specific use is acceptable for a given project.

What an ESR Does and Does Not Do

What an ESR Provides

  • Code-referenced design values such as allowable loads
  • Approved installation methods
  • Conditions under which the product may be used

What an ESR Does Not Provide

  • A project-specific design
  • Verification that your exact application meets all loads
  • Coordination with other building systems
  • Approval outside the stated conditions of use

Using a product outside the ESR's limits may require additional justification.

Common Situations Where ESRs Are Referenced

  • Adhesive or expansion anchors in concrete or masonry
  • Structural connectors such as hangers, brackets, and hold-downs
  • Proprietary wall, roof, or floor systems
  • Specialty fasteners or retrofit hardware
  • Post-installed anchorage and retrofit details

These products often rely on ESR data for sizing and installation.

When a Professional Engineer May Be Helpful

Interpreting ESR Design Values

ESRs include tables and conditions that must be applied correctly. A PE can:

  • Translate ESR values into project-specific loads
  • Check governing limit states such as pullout and breakout
  • Apply appropriate safety factors and load combinations

Project-Specific Conditions Differ from the ESR

If your project involves:

  • Edge distances or spacing outside ESR limits
  • Different base materials or strengths
  • Combined loading (tension plus shear)
  • Unusual geometry or constraints

A PE can evaluate whether the application is acceptable or recommend alternatives.

Supporting Permit Submittals

Some jurisdictions require sealed calculations or details when:

  • Using proprietary systems in structural applications
  • Submitting alternate materials or methods
  • Demonstrating compliance for non-prescriptive designs

Engineering documentation can help streamline review.

Evaluating Existing Conditions

For retrofit or repair work, a PE may:

  • Assess existing concrete or substrate condition
  • Determine feasible anchor types and embedment
  • Verify capacity for added loads

Resolving Field Questions or Changes

If conditions change during construction, such as slab thickness differing from plans, a PE can:

  • Re-evaluate anchor sizing
  • Provide revised details
  • Document changes for the record

Coordination with Contractors and Inspectors

Successful use of ESR-based products depends on:

  • Correct installation per the ESR, such as hole cleaning, torque, and embedment
  • Documentation included in the permit set
  • Inspection alignment with ESR conditions

Engineers, contractors, and inspectors each play a role in ensuring compliance.

Practical Tips for Using ESRs

  • Include the relevant ESR in your permit submittal
  • Verify that the edition of the code referenced aligns with the jurisdiction
  • Check conditions of use carefully, including temperature, installation method, and substrate
  • Confirm that edge distances and spacing are achievable in the field
  • Coordinate early if deviations are anticipated

Closing

ICC-ES reports can be valuable tools for demonstrating code compliance when proprietary products are used in Missouri projects. When the application is non-standard, field conditions differ, or permitting requires additional documentation, involvement from a Professional Engineer can help translate ESR information into project-specific analysis and clear supporting details.

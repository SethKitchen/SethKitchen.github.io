Engineering Review for Custom Automation and Controls Projects in Missouri

July 8, 2026

Custom automation and controls projects sit at the intersection of mechanical equipment, electrical power, programmable logic, and operational safety. In Missouri, manufacturers, agricultural operations, warehouses, and specialty fabricators routinely deploy conveyors, PLCs, sensors, actuators, and one-off machinery that does not fit neatly into prescriptive code categories.

System integrators and controls contractors often lead design and installation. However, there are situations where independent engineering review — from a licensed Professional Engineer (PE) — adds value: validating loads and anchorage, reviewing safety interlocks, supporting permit documentation, or providing a technical opinion when performance or liability questions arise.

This article provides general information about engineering review for custom automation and controls projects in Missouri.

What Counts as a Custom Automation or Controls Project?

These projects typically involve engineered integration rather than off-the-shelf installation alone. Examples include:

  • PLC-controlled conveyor lines, sortation systems, and material handling equipment
  • Custom-built production machinery, fixtures, and test stands
  • Automated packaging, palletizing, or batch processing lines
  • Robotic work cells with guarding, interlocks, and end effectors
  • Agricultural automation — grain handling, feed systems, irrigation controls, and process monitoring
  • Building or facility controls integrating HVAC, pumps, and process equipment
  • Retrofits that add sensors, VFDs, or logic to existing mechanical systems

The common thread is coordination across disciplines: mechanical loads and motion, electrical supply and controls, and software logic that governs safe operation.

Integrator Work vs. Engineering Review

Controls integrators and machine builders provide essential design, programming, and commissioning services. Engineering review is complementary — not a replacement — and may be appropriate when:

  • An owner or lender wants independent verification before capital expenditure
  • Permit authorities request sealed documentation for structural supports, anchorage, or electrical load
  • A system underperforms, faults repeatedly, or raises safety concerns after startup
  • Contract disputes arise over design responsibility or scope adequacy
  • Insurance or loss events require technical analysis of controls logic or equipment integration
  • Non-standard machinery must be evaluated against applicable standards and good engineering practice

Review scope can range from a focused desktop evaluation to broader multi-discipline analysis depending on project complexity.

Key Engineering Considerations

Mechanical Loads and Support

Conveyors, machinery, and automated equipment impose static and dynamic loads on floors, mezzanines, and structural supports. Engineering review may address:

  • Equipment weight, vibration, and impact loading
  • Anchorage and bolt patterns to slabs or steel
  • Guardrail and platform loading where personnel access exists
  • Deflection limits affecting alignment of belts, chains, or rollers

Electrical Power and Controls

Automation projects add motor loads, VFDs, control panels, and branch circuits. Review may consider:

  • Service and panel capacity for added equipment
  • Motor starting, harmonic, and coordination concerns
  • Panel layout, disconnecting means, and overcurrent protection
  • Grounding, bonding, and wiring methods for industrial environments

PLC Logic, Interlocks, and Safety

Programmable logic controllers govern sequence, timing, and fault response. Engineering review may evaluate whether:

  • Safety interlocks and emergency stops appear adequately incorporated in the control concept
  • Guarding and access restrictions align with hazardous motion zones
  • Failure modes — power loss, sensor fault, jam — are addressed in system design documentation
  • Control narratives and I/O lists match observed or intended operation

Detailed PLC programming review may be limited by proprietary code access; review often focuses on documented logic, schematics, and functional descriptions rather than line-by-line code audit unless scope and access support deeper analysis.

Custom Machinery and Conveyors

One-off machines and conveyor assemblies may lack manufacturer engineering packages. A PE may review:

  • Frame and member sizing for expected loads
  • Drive selection, belt/chain tension, and sprocket alignment
  • Guarding, pinch points, and maintenance access
  • Integration with upstream and downstream equipment

Common Project Stages Where Review Helps

  • Concept and layout — Early review of loads, clearances, and utility requirements before fabrication orders
  • Design submittal — Review of integrator drawings, control schematics, and equipment schedules before permit or purchase
  • Pre-startup — Verification that installed conditions align with documented design intent
  • Post-incident — Forensic evaluation after jam, injury, fire, or equipment failure involving automated systems
  • Expansion or retrofit — Review when new automation is added to existing lines with limited spare capacity

Permits, Inspections, and Documentation

Custom automation projects in Missouri may trigger building, electrical, or mechanical permit requirements depending on scope and jurisdiction. Engineering documentation may support:

  • Sealed structural details for equipment supports and platforms
  • Electrical load calculations for panel and service changes
  • Technical summaries for plan review when prescriptive paths do not apply
  • As-built documentation after field changes during installation

Requirements vary by municipality and project type. Early confirmation with the local building department helps avoid retrofit documentation after equipment is installed.

Missouri Industry Context

Automation and controls work appears across many Missouri sectors:

  • Manufacturing and fabrication facilities in St. Louis, Kansas City, Springfield, and regional industrial parks
  • Warehousing and distribution centers with conveyor and sortation systems
  • Food processing, agricultural processing, and grain handling operations
  • Custom machine shops building specialty production equipment
  • Facility upgrades adding monitoring, batch control, or remote operation

Engineering review scope should match the sector — a warehouse conveyor retrofit raises different questions than a custom chemical batching skid or an agricultural feed system.

When a Professional Engineer May Be Involved

A Missouri PE may be engaged when:

  • Sealed drawings or calculations are required for permit submittal
  • Independent review of integrator or vendor design is requested
  • Structural or electrical adequacy of supports and power supply is in question
  • A technical memo is needed for ownership, insurance, or dispute resolution
  • Post-failure analysis is needed to evaluate causation involving controls or machinery
  • Multi-discipline coordination is required across mechanical, electrical, and controls scope

Documents are sealed when appropriate and when scope supports responsible charge under Missouri engineering rules.

What to Provide for an Engineering Review

  • Project description, location, and intended operation
  • Equipment lists, motor schedules, and control panel one-line diagrams if available
  • Layout drawings, conveyor profiles, and anchorage details
  • PLC I/O lists, control narratives, or functional descriptions
  • Photos and measurements of existing conditions for retrofits
  • Any permit comments, incident reports, or performance concerns

Clear scope definition at intake helps align review depth with project needs and fee.

Practical Guidance

  • Define mechanical, electrical, and controls responsibility before fabrication begins
  • Request documentation from integrators in reviewable form — not only as-built after startup
  • Confirm permit triggers early for structural supports and electrical work
  • Consider independent engineering review for high-capital or safety-critical automation
  • Preserve control narratives and wiring diagrams for future troubleshooting and modification

Closing

Custom automation and controls projects in Missouri combine mechanical equipment, electrical power, and programmable logic in ways that often exceed prescriptive code paths. Engineering review can help validate loads, supports, power requirements, and documented control concepts — and provide independent technical input when permits, performance, or disputes require it.

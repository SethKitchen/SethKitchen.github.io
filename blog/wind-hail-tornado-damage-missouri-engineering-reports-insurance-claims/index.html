Wind, Hail, and Tornado Damage in Missouri: When Engineering Reports Help Insurance Claims

June 3, 2026

Missouri experiences severe thunderstorms, hail, high winds, and tornadoes throughout much of the year. After a storm event, property owners often face urgent questions about safety, repair scope, and insurance coverage.

Visible roof or siding damage may be obvious, but storm events can also affect structural framing, connections, and building performance in ways that are not immediately apparent. In some situations, a licensed Professional Engineer (PE) may be asked to evaluate conditions and prepare a written engineering report that helps inform claim review and repair decisions.

This article provides general information about wind, hail, and tornado-related damage in Missouri and when engineering reports may be helpful in insurance-related situations.

How Wind, Hail, and Tornado Events Affect Buildings

Different storm mechanisms can produce different types of damage:

  • High winds may uplift roofing, displace sheathing, bend or detach framing members, and overload connections
  • Hail may puncture or dent roofing and cladding, damage skylights or vents, and in severe cases affect underlying materials
  • Tornadoes and microbursts may cause localized structural failure, wall displacement, roof loss, or partial collapse
  • Wind-driven rain may allow water intrusion that affects insulation, sheathing, or framing over time
  • Flying debris may cause impact damage to roofs, walls, windows, and attached structures

The severity of damage depends on storm intensity, building configuration, age, maintenance, and local exposure conditions.

Damage That May Not Be Obvious at First

After a storm, some conditions may require closer evaluation:

  • Roof truss or rafter damage concealed by finishes
  • Sheathing nail withdrawal or panel buckling
  • Connection loosening or fastener failure
  • Wall or foundation movement following lateral loading
  • Garage door or large-opening failures affecting load paths
  • Moisture intrusion behind repaired exterior surfaces

Because storm damage can be progressive—especially when water intrusion is involved—early documentation and technical evaluation may help preserve information useful for both safety and claim review.

Safety Considerations After a Storm

Before reoccupying or beginning repairs, it is important to understand whether the building remains safe. Concerns may include:

  • Partial roof loss or unstable framing
  • Leaning walls or displaced structural members
  • Compromised floor or ceiling systems
  • Fall hazards from damaged materials or equipment
  • Electrical or gas system damage (which may require other qualified professionals)

An engineering evaluation can help determine whether temporary stabilization, restricted access, or further investigation is appropriate before repair work begins.

Why Engineering Evaluation May Be Requested for Claims

Insurance claims involving property damage often require determining:

  • What damage is present
  • Whether observed conditions are consistent with the reported event
  • Whether damage affects structural performance or safety
  • What type of repair may be appropriate
  • Whether pre-existing conditions contributed to the outcome

When structural performance is in question, a Professional Engineer may provide a technical opinion based on site observations, available documentation, and engineering principles.

Types of Storm-Related Damage a PE May Evaluate

A Missouri-licensed Professional Engineer may be involved in evaluating conditions such as:

  • Roof framing, sheathing, or truss damage
  • Wall framing displacement or connection failure
  • Garage, porch, or accessory structure damage
  • Foundation or slab distress following lateral loads
  • Damage to load-bearing elements from impact or debris
  • Structural concerns following partial building envelope loss

Not all storm claims require engineering input. Involvement may be appropriate when structural significance, repair scope, or safety is unclear.

What an Engineering Report May Include

Depending on scope and available information, an engineering evaluation may include:

  • Site observations of affected areas
  • Documentation of visible structural conditions
  • Review of photos, prior reports, or plans if available
  • Assessment of whether observed damage appears consistent with reported causes
  • Opinion regarding structural significance
  • General recommendations for repair, replacement, or further investigation

The level of detail varies depending on the assignment, building type, and how much information is available at the time of review.

Role of Engineering in the Insurance Claims Process

It is important to understand the role of a Professional Engineer in this context:

  • Engineers provide technical opinions based on observed conditions and engineering judgment
  • They may help clarify whether damage appears related to a particular event or condition
  • They may comment on whether structural repair appears warranted
  • They may help distinguish cosmetic damage from performance-related concerns

Engineers do not approve or deny insurance claims. Coverage determinations are made by the insurer based on policy terms and other factors.

When a Professional Engineer May Be Helpful

In Missouri, a PE may be involved when:

  • There is disagreement about whether damage is structural
  • The extent of storm-related damage is unclear
  • A contractor's repair scope needs technical support
  • An insurer or property owner requests an independent evaluation
  • A written report is needed to document conditions
  • Structural safety or long-term performance is a concern after wind, hail, or tornado exposure

Engineering input can provide an objective, technically grounded perspective that supports informed repair and claim discussions.

Coordination with Other Professionals

Storm-damaged buildings are often evaluated by multiple parties, including:

  • Insurance adjusters and claim representatives
  • Roofing and restoration contractors
  • Building officials
  • Environmental or remediation professionals (when moisture is involved)

Engineering input focuses on structural performance and technical evaluation, while other professionals address coverage, restoration logistics, and regulatory compliance.

Permit and Reconstruction Considerations in Missouri

Repairing storm-damaged structures typically requires permits from the local jurisdiction. In some cases:

  • Engineered drawings may be required for structural repairs or replacements
  • Damaged components should be evaluated before removal when documentation may be needed
  • Inspectors may require documentation demonstrating that repairs restore code-aligned performance

Early engineering involvement can help align repair plans with permit requirements and reduce delays during reconstruction.

Practical Guidance for Property Owners

After a wind, hail, or tornado event:

  • Limit access to damaged areas until conditions are understood
  • Document conditions with photographs as soon as it is safe
  • Avoid removing structural elements before evaluation when documentation may matter
  • Coordinate with your insurer and local building department
  • Consider whether engineering review may help clarify technical issues before repairs begin
  • Address temporary weather protection promptly to limit secondary water damage

Early documentation can help preserve information that may be important for both repair planning and claim review.

Closing

Wind, hail, and tornado events can affect Missouri buildings in ways that are not always visible at first glance. Engineering reports may help clarify structural conditions, support repair decisions, and inform insurance-related discussions when technical questions arise.

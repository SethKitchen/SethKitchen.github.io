What Is a Sunshine Request in Missouri and How Public Records Can Support Engineering Reports

May 18, 2026

Engineering reports often rely on more than what is physically observable during a site visit. Historical permits, approved drawings, inspection records, and prior correspondence held by public agencies can fill critical gaps—especially when original construction documents are unavailable, dimensions are missing, or the history of a structure is unclear.

In Missouri, the Sunshine Law gives the public a legal right to request these records from government bodies. This article provides general information about what a Sunshine request is, what kinds of records may be available, and how that information can support engineering analysis.

What Is the Missouri Sunshine Law?

Missouri's Sunshine Law (Chapter 610, RSMo) establishes a general right of public access to government records and meetings. The statute presumes that public records are open unless a specific exemption applies.

A "Sunshine request" is simply a written request submitted to a public governmental body asking for records it holds. The request can be directed to:

  • City or county building departments
  • Planning and zoning offices
  • Missouri Department of Transportation (MoDOT) district offices
  • Public works or engineering departments
  • Other state or local agencies that maintain relevant records

Agencies must acknowledge a request within three business days and must respond—either by providing records or stating a basis for withholding them—within a reasonable time. Some agencies charge fees for search time, copying, or document preparation.

What Types of Records Can Be Requested?

The types of records available depend on the agency and the subject matter, but engineering-relevant requests commonly target:

Building Permits and Permit History

Building departments maintain permit records that may include application details, approved scope of work, fee receipts, and status notes. For older structures, permit records can establish when additions or modifications were made and whether they were authorized.

Approved Construction Drawings

Many jurisdictions retain copies of drawings submitted with permit applications. These may include architectural or structural plans, site plans, or mechanical and electrical layouts that were reviewed and stamped during the original permit process. Where original drawings no longer exist in private hands, the agency's copy may be the only remaining record.

Inspection Records

Inspection records document when work was inspected, which inspections passed or failed, and whether final approval was granted. These records can be useful when there is a question about whether work was completed as permitted or whether specific conditions were observed during construction.

Correspondence and Violation Notices

Agencies may retain letters, emails, stop work orders, or code violation notices related to a property. This correspondence can establish a timeline of known issues, agency findings, or required remediation—information that may not be reflected in drawings or inspection cards alone.

Right-of-Way and Infrastructure Records

For projects involving driveways, utilities, or site improvements near public roads, MoDOT and county or city public works offices may hold access permits, right-of-way maps, utility as-builts, or drainage records relevant to the property.

How Public Records Can Support Engineering Analysis

Engineering reports are only as strong as the information they are based on. When a site visit reveals uncertainty—about original construction, prior modifications, or existing conditions—public records can help establish a more complete factual record.

Recovering Missing Dimensions

Original drawings obtained through a Sunshine request may include dimensions that are no longer accessible in the field—buried elements, covered framing, or sealed assemblies. Where current conditions cannot be directly measured, approved permit drawings can provide a documented basis for engineering assumptions, provided the engineer notes how that information was obtained and its limitations.

Establishing Historical Context

Permit history can help establish when a structure or system was built, what code edition was in effect at the time, and whether subsequent modifications were authorized. This context is relevant when evaluating whether conditions are consistent with original design intent or represent deviations from approved work.

Identifying Prior Issues or Agency Findings

If a building department previously issued a violation notice or required corrective action, those records may be relevant to understanding the current condition of a structure. Similarly, records showing that prior remediation was required but never formally closed out can be significant in forensic or dispute contexts.

Supporting Expert Reports and Litigation

In matters involving disputes, insurance claims, or litigation, engineering opinions carry more weight when they are grounded in documented evidence rather than assumptions alone. Public records obtained through the Sunshine Law are government documents with a traceable chain of custody, which can strengthen the evidentiary foundation of a technical report.

Practical Considerations When Making a Sunshine Request

A few practical points are worth keeping in mind:

  • Requests should be specific enough that the agency can identify the records sought. Including the property address, parcel number, approximate date range, and the type of records requested helps narrow the search.
  • Not all records are retained indefinitely. Older permits or drawings may have been purged according to agency retention schedules, particularly for routine residential work.
  • Some records are exempt from disclosure under the Sunshine Law, including certain law enforcement records, personnel files, and attorney-client communications. Most permit and inspection records, however, are presumed open.
  • Response timelines vary by agency and workload. Requests involving large volumes of records or off-site archives may take longer to fulfill.
  • Fees may apply for search time and copying. Missouri law allows agencies to charge reasonable fees, though requests are sometimes fulfilled at no cost for small volumes of records.

How a Professional Engineer May Help

A Professional Engineer can assist in identifying what records are likely to exist and how to use them once obtained. In the context of an engineering engagement, a PE may:

  • Review permit drawings and inspection records to compare against current field conditions
  • Identify discrepancies between approved plans and as-built conditions
  • Use historical dimensions or specifications from permit documents as a documented basis for engineering assumptions
  • Incorporate public record findings into a report in a way that clearly identifies the source and any limitations
  • Advise on what additional records might be worth pursuing based on the scope of the analysis

Public records do not replace physical investigation, but they can significantly improve the quality and defensibility of an engineering report when field access is limited or records are otherwise unavailable.

Closing

Missouri's Sunshine Law provides a valuable tool for recovering information that may no longer exist in private files—approved drawings, inspection records, permit history, and agency correspondence that can add historical context and fill factual gaps in engineering analysis.

How Engineers Evaluate Causation in Failure Analysis

Understanding Why a System Failed

Failure analysis is not limited to identifying what went wrong. A central objective is determining causation—why the failure occurred and what factors materially contributed to the outcome.

In professional engineering practice, causation is evaluated through structured analysis grounded in evidence, engineering principles, and accepted methodology. This process is especially important in forensic and expert witness contexts, where conclusions may be relied upon by courts, insurers, or other decision-makers.

Defining Failure and Scope

Before causation can be evaluated, the engineer must define:

  • What constitutes “failure” in the specific context
  • The performance criteria or expectations involved
  • The system boundaries and time frame under consideration

Failure may involve physical damage, loss of function, deviation from specifications, or inability to meet operational requirements. Clear definition prevents conclusions from drifting beyond the facts.

Evidence Collection and Preservation

Causation analysis depends on reliable evidence. Engineers typically begin by gathering and reviewing available materials, which may include:

  • Drawings, specifications, and design documents
  • Maintenance and inspection records
  • Operational data and logs
  • Photographs, videos, or physical artifacts
  • Witness statements or timelines

Where possible, evidence is preserved to avoid altering or obscuring relevant conditions.

Identifying Potential Failure Modes

Engineers then identify plausible failure modes—mechanisms by which the system could have failed. This may involve:

  • Reviewing design assumptions
  • Evaluating loading and operating conditions
  • Considering environmental factors
  • Examining human or procedural interactions
  • Assessing degradation over time

At this stage, multiple hypotheses are often considered.

Evaluating Contributing Factors

Causation in engineering is rarely singular. Engineers distinguish between:

  • Primary causes that directly led to failure
  • Contributing factors that increased likelihood or severity
  • Non-causal conditions present but not materially relevant

This distinction is critical in forensic contexts, where responsibility and liability may hinge on material contribution rather than mere association.

Use of Engineering Principles and Standards

Engineering causation analysis relies on established principles, such as:

  • Mechanics and material behavior
  • Control system response
  • Load paths and redundancy
  • Failure progression and sequence

Applicable standards and codes are often used as reference points, not as automatic determinants of causation. Deviation from a standard does not necessarily cause failure, and compliance does not guarantee immunity from it.

Testing, Modeling, and Reconstruction

Where appropriate, engineers may use:

  • Analytical calculations
  • Computer modeling or simulation
  • Laboratory testing
  • Scenario reconstruction

These tools help evaluate whether proposed causes are consistent with observed outcomes. Assumptions and limitations must be clearly stated.

Eliminating Alternative Explanations

A key element of causation analysis is the systematic elimination of alternative explanations. Engineers assess whether:

  • Evidence supports or contradicts each hypothesis
  • Proposed causes are physically or technically plausible
  • Timing and sequence align with observed damage

Conclusions are strengthened when reasonable alternatives are addressed and ruled out based on evidence.

Communicating Causation Clearly

Engineering conclusions must be communicated in a way that is accurate, transparent, and understandable to non-engineers.

Effective communication includes:

  • Clear separation of facts, assumptions, and opinions
  • Explanation of reasoning and methodology
  • Identification of uncertainties or data gaps
  • Avoidance of speculation beyond available evidence

In expert witness settings, clarity is as important as technical rigor.

Limits of Engineering Causation Analysis

Engineering analysis does not determine legal responsibility or fault. Engineers evaluate technical causation, not legal conclusions. Where evidence is incomplete or ambiguous, responsible engineers acknowledge limitations rather than overstate certainty.

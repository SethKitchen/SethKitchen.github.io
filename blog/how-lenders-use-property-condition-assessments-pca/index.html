How Lenders Use Property Condition Assessments (PCAs)

February 4, 2026

Why PCAs Matter in Real Estate Financing

Property Condition Assessments (PCAs) are commonly used by lenders during underwriting and due diligence for commercial and multifamily real estate transactions. While the format of PCA reports varies by firm and scope, their role in lending decisions is fairly consistent: PCAs help lenders understand physical risks associated with a property at the time of financing.

This article provides general information about how PCAs are used in lending and does not constitute financial, legal, or engineering advice.

Risk Identification, Not Just Documentation

From a lender’s perspective, a PCA is primarily a risk management tool. It helps identify:

  • Material physical deficiencies
  • Conditions that may affect habitability, safety, or continued operation
  • Deferred maintenance
  • Near-term capital needs that could impact cash flow

The goal is not to certify that a property is “good” or “bad,” but to surface physical risks that could affect the lender’s collateral over the loan term.

How PCA Findings Affect Underwriting

PCA findings can influence underwriting in several ways:

  • Holdbacks and reserves: Lenders may require repair escrows or reserves for identified deficiencies.
  • Loan terms: Significant condition issues may affect loan-to-value ratios, covenants, or pricing.
  • Closing conditions: Certain deficiencies may need to be corrected prior to closing.
  • Ongoing monitoring: Some lenders use PCA findings to define post-closing repair timelines.

Engineering Judgment in PCAs

While many PCAs are observational, engineering judgment may be applied when evaluating:

  • Structural components
  • Building envelope performance
  • Life-safety related systems
  • Observed distress that requires technical interpretation

When professional engineering judgment is applied, the scope and limitations should be clearly documented, and licensure requirements apply based on jurisdiction and scope of services.

Limitations of PCAs in Lending Contexts

PCAs are not comprehensive building investigations. Common limitations include:

  • No destructive testing
  • Limited access to concealed conditions
  • Reliance on visual observations
  • No guarantee of future performance

Lenders typically understand these limitations and use PCAs as one input among many in risk assessment.

PCA vs. CNA in Financing

Lenders may request PCAs, CNAs, or both depending on transaction structure:

  • PCA: snapshot of current condition
  • CNA: projection of future capital needs over the loan term

For longer-term financing, capital planning information may be more relevant to underwriting.

Practical Takeaways for Property Owners and Buyers

When a PCA is required by a lender:

  • Provide reasonable access to building areas and documentation
  • Expect that deficiencies will be documented neutrally
  • Use the findings as a planning tool, not just a transaction hurdle
  • Clarify scope early so the report aligns with the lender’s expectations

Unpermitted Work in Missouri: Can It Be Legalized, and When Does Engineering Documentation Help?

July 6, 2026

Unpermitted work is one of the most common problems property owners and buyers encounter in Missouri. A deck built without a permit, a basement finish never inspected, a garage conversion completed years ago, or structural changes made during a remodel — these situations surface during sales, insurance reviews, neighbor complaints, or building department inspections.

The immediate question is usually practical: can the work be legalized, or must it be removed? The answer depends on the jurisdiction, the type of work, code requirements in effect, and whether the construction can be demonstrated to meet applicable standards — sometimes with engineering documentation.

This article provides general information about unpermitted work in Missouri and when a licensed Professional Engineer (PE) may help support legalization or compliance resolution.

What Counts as Unpermitted Work?

Unpermitted work generally means construction, alteration, or system installation that required a building or trade permit but was completed without one, or without required inspections. Common examples in Missouri include:

  • Decks, porches, and attached structures
  • Basement or attic finishing and bedroom additions
  • Garage conversions and accessory dwelling spaces
  • Structural wall removals, headers, and framing changes
  • Electrical panel upgrades and generator installations
  • HVAC replacements or duct modifications
  • Sheds, carports, and pole barns above permit thresholds
  • Foundation repairs or retaining walls

Permit requirements vary by city, county, and project type. Work that did not require a permit when originally built may still raise questions if subsequent code changes or occupancy use changed.

How Unpermitted Work Is Often Discovered

  • Real estate disclosures or buyer inspections during a sale
  • Appraiser or lender questions about finished square footage
  • Neighbor complaints or visible construction from the street
  • Building department investigation after a related permit application
  • Stop work orders on adjacent or follow-on projects
  • Insurance claim inspections that reveal undocumented alterations

Discovery does not always mean immediate penalties, but it usually triggers a requirement to address compliance before the matter can be closed.

Can Unpermitted Work Be Legalized in Missouri?

In many Missouri jurisdictions, unpermitted work can sometimes be legalized through a retroactive or "after-the-fact" permit process — but legalization is not guaranteed. Outcomes typically fall into one of these paths:

  • Retroactive permit approval — The owner submits plans, pays fees, passes inspections, and receives a permit covering work already completed
  • Conditional approval with corrections — The jurisdiction allows legalization if specific upgrades, repairs, or documentation are provided
  • Partial legalization — Some elements are approved while others must be modified or removed
  • Denial or removal order — Work cannot be brought into compliance and must be demolished or reversed

Jurisdictions differ in how flexible they are with after-the-fact permits. Factors may include how long ago the work was done, whether it is structurally sound, zoning compliance, and whether required inspections can still be performed meaningfully.

Stop Work Orders and Enforcement

When unpermitted work is active or recently discovered, building departments may issue a stop work order halting further construction until compliance is resolved. Stop orders may also apply when:

  • Work is underway without a permit
  • Completed work is flagged during a separate permit review
  • Inspections reveal code violations or unsafe conditions
  • Construction does not match previously approved plans

Resolving a stop order usually requires a defined compliance plan — which may include retroactive permits, engineered documentation, corrective construction, and reinspection. The specific steps depend on what the building official requires.

The Retroactive Permit Process (General Overview)

While procedures vary by municipality, a retroactive permit process often involves:

  1. Contacting the local building department to describe the work
  2. Determining whether a retroactive permit is available for that project type
  3. Submitting plans, photos, and supporting documentation
  4. Paying applicable permit and inspection fees
  5. Opening concealed areas if inspectors require visibility into framing, wiring, or connections
  6. Passing required inspections
  7. Receiving final approval or certificate of occupancy if applicable

Plan reviewers may treat retroactive submittals similarly to new permit applications — including comment letters and resubmittals when information is incomplete or technically deficient.

When Engineering Documentation Helps

Building officials often require more than owner statements when unpermitted work involves structural, electrical, or non-prescriptive construction. A Professional Engineer may help by providing:

  • As-built evaluation of existing framing, headers, foundations, or connections
  • Calculations demonstrating adequate capacity for observed construction
  • Sealed drawings or sketches documenting existing conditions and any required corrections
  • Written summary letters explaining scope, assumptions, and findings for plan review
  • Recommendations for corrective work when deficiencies are identified
  • Review of contractor-prepared details that need PE verification before sealing

Engineering documentation is commonly requested for decks, wall openings, additions, garage conversions, and any work where load path or lateral resistance is not obvious from visual inspection alone.

Common Scenarios Where PE Involvement Arises

Decks and Porches

Unpermitted decks are frequently flagged during home sales. Reviewers may ask for post footing depth, ledger attachment, joist sizing, guardrail height, and lateral connection details. Engineering evaluation of existing construction may support retroactive approval or identify corrections needed.

Structural Modifications

Removed walls, enlarged openings, and modified framing without permits often require demonstration that headers, posts, and connections are adequate. This is a common trigger for sealed calculations and summary letters.

Basement and Garage Conversions

Finished spaces may raise egress, ceiling height, fire separation, and structural questions. Engineering may address structural elements while other disciplines cover code-specific life-safety requirements.

Accessory Structures

Pole barns, carports, and large sheds built without permits may need wind and snow load documentation, foundation verification, and anchorage review for legalization.

Plan Review Comments on Retroactive Submittals

Retroactive permit applications are not exempt from plan review. Reviewers may issue comments similar to new construction submittals, such as:

  • Provide engineered details for existing header at opening
  • Submit footing depth verification or foundation details
  • Demonstrate lateral bracing or connection capacity
  • Provide as-built drawings with dimensions and member sizes
  • Open finishes at connections for field inspection before approval

Responding to these comments often requires measured field conditions and technical documentation — not just resubmitting the same incomplete package.

Real Estate and Disclosure Considerations

Unpermitted work frequently surfaces during property transactions. Buyers, lenders, and title companies may ask whether work was permitted and inspected. Legalization before closing — or escrow holdbacks for post-closing compliance — is sometimes negotiated.

Engineering documentation alone does not replace a permit, but it may support the legalization process and give buyers confidence that structural concerns have been technically reviewed. Permit status and disclosure obligations are legal matters for the parties and their counsel to address.

When Legalization May Not Be Possible

Engineering review may conclude — or a building official may determine — that unpermitted work cannot be legalized without substantial reconstruction or removal. Examples include:

  • Work that violates zoning setbacks, height limits, or lot coverage
  • Structural conditions that cannot be economically corrected
  • Construction that cannot be inspected because finishes cannot reasonably be removed
  • Life-safety deficiencies that exceed practical retrofit scope
  • Jurisdictions that do not offer retroactive permits for certain work types

Early technical evaluation can help owners understand feasibility before investing in a full permit application.

Practical Guidance for Property Owners

  • Contact the local building department before assuming work must be demolished
  • Gather photos, measurements, and any existing sketches of the work
  • Ask specifically whether a retroactive permit path exists for your project type
  • Do not conceal or cover work that may need inspection
  • Engage a PE early when reviewers request engineered documentation
  • Budget for permit fees, possible corrective construction, and reinspection
  • Address unpermitted work before listing a property when possible

Closing

Unpermitted work in Missouri can sometimes be legalized through retroactive permits, inspections, and corrective construction — but outcomes depend on jurisdiction, work type, and whether the construction can be demonstrated to meet applicable standards. Professional Engineer documentation may help when structural or technical adequacy must be shown for plan review, stop order resolution, or real estate compliance.

Root Cause vs. Legal Cause: An Engineering Perspective

Why the Distinction Matters

In disputes involving technical failures, the terms root cause and legal cause are sometimes used interchangeably. In practice, they represent different concepts. Understanding the distinction is important for attorneys, courts, and engineers alike.

Professional engineers evaluate technical causation. Courts evaluate legal responsibility. While related, these analyses serve different purposes.

What Engineers Mean by Root Cause

In engineering practice, a root cause is the underlying technical reason a failure occurred. Identifying it typically involves:

  • Defining failure criteria
  • Examining system behavior and conditions
  • Reviewing assumptions and design decisions
  • Evaluating failure modes and sequences

A root cause analysis seeks to explain why a system did not perform as intended from an engineering standpoint.

There may be more than one contributing technical factor, but the root cause is generally the factor without which the failure would not have occurred.

Legal Cause Is a Separate Question

Legal cause—often discussed as proximate cause—is a legal concept. Courts evaluate whether a particular act or omission is sufficiently connected to an outcome to warrant responsibility under the law.

Legal causation may consider factors such as:

  • Foreseeability
  • Intervening events
  • Allocation of responsibility
  • Policy considerations

These considerations extend beyond technical analysis.

How Engineering Analysis Informs Legal Decisions

Engineering causation analysis provides courts with a factual and technical foundation. Courts may rely on engineering testimony to understand:

  • How a system was designed to function
  • What conditions existed at the time of failure
  • Whether technical standards were met
  • Which factors materially contributed to the outcome

However, engineering opinions do not determine legal outcomes on their own.

Multiple Causes and Contributing Factors

Engineering failures often involve multiple contributing factors. Engineers distinguish between:

  • Primary technical causes
  • Secondary or contributing conditions
  • Factors that were present but not causally significant

Legal frameworks may treat these factors differently when assigning responsibility.

Importance of Clear Scope in Expert Testimony

Engineering experts must clearly define the scope of their opinions. Testimony should focus on technical causation and avoid legal conclusions.

Clear scope helps:

  • Preserve credibility
  • Avoid confusion for the court
  • Ensure testimony remains within professional competence

Courts are generally skeptical of experts who blur the line between engineering analysis and legal judgment.

Communicating Technical Causation Effectively

For engineering analysis to be useful in legal settings, it must be communicated clearly. Effective testimony:

  • Separates facts from opinions
  • Explains assumptions and limitations
  • Uses engineering principles consistently
  • Avoids speculation beyond available evidence

Clear communication allows legal decision-makers to properly weigh technical findings.

Professional Responsibility and Ethics

Professional engineers are bound by ethical obligations to provide honest, objective opinions. Root cause analysis must be grounded in evidence and professional judgment, regardless of how the conclusions may affect the parties involved.

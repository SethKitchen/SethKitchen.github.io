The Role of a Professional Engineer as an Expert Witness in Missouri

Technical Expertise in Legal and Dispute Contexts

Engineering issues frequently arise in legal disputes, insurance claims, and regulatory proceedings. When technical questions are central to a matter, courts and attorneys may rely on expert testimony to understand engineering principles, system behavior, and technical causation.

In Missouri, a licensed Professional Engineer may serve as an expert witness when the subject matter falls within the engineer’s area of competence and the opinions offered are based on sufficient technical analysis.

This article describes the role of an engineering expert witness and the professional responsibilities that accompany that role.

What an Engineering Expert Witness Does

An engineering expert witness provides independent technical opinions to assist the court or other decision-makers. This role is distinct from advocacy and is grounded in objective engineering analysis.

Typical expert witness activities may include:

  • Reviewing technical documents, drawings, and records
  • Evaluating system design, operation, and failure modes
  • Analyzing data, logs, and test results
  • Preparing written expert reports
  • Providing deposition or trial testimony

The expert’s duty is to the facts and engineering principles, not to a particular outcome.

Independence and Professional Judgment

Professional engineers serving as expert witnesses are ethically obligated to remain independent and unbiased. Opinions must be formed through analysis rather than instruction from counsel or clients.

Key elements of professional judgment in expert work include:

  • Clear distinction between facts, assumptions, and opinions
  • Use of accepted engineering methods
  • Disclosure of limitations or uncertainties
  • Avoidance of conclusions beyond available evidence

Maintaining independence is essential for credibility and admissibility.

Engineering Expertise and Modern Systems

Many disputes today involve complex systems that include software, control logic, or distributed components. Expert evaluation of such systems often requires understanding both theoretical design and real-world operation.

Expert witness work in these cases may involve:

  • Reconstruction of system behavior over time
  • Evaluation of control or decision logic
  • Analysis of interactions between software and physical components
  • Review of verification and validation practices

Engineering expertise must be applied in a manner that is clear and accessible to non-technical audiences without oversimplifying the underlying analysis.

Legal Standards and Professional Responsibility

While legal standards for expert testimony vary by jurisdiction, professional engineers are governed by ethical and licensure obligations regardless of venue.

In Missouri, engineers must:

  • Practice within their area of competence
  • Avoid misleading statements or omissions
  • Maintain honesty and objectivity
  • Accept responsibility for opinions offered

Failure to adhere to these principles can affect both professional credibility and licensure.

When an Engineering Expert May Be Needed

An engineering expert witness may be appropriate when:

  • Technical causation is disputed
  • Engineering standards or practices are questioned
  • System behavior is not readily understood by non-engineers
  • Independent analysis is required to inform decision-making

Early technical evaluation can often clarify issues and narrow the scope of disputes.

Closing

This article is intended to provide general information about the role of Professional Engineers as expert witnesses in Missouri. It does not constitute engineering advice or establish a professional engagement.

Engineering expert testimony relies on careful analysis, clear reasoning, and professional integrity.

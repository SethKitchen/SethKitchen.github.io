What Does an Engineering Expert Witness Do?

The Role of Engineering Expertise in Legal Matters

Engineering expert witnesses play a critical role in legal disputes involving technical, scientific, or engineering issues. Their function is not to advocate for a party, but to provide independent, objective engineering analysis and opinion to assist the court in understanding complex matters.

In Missouri, expert witnesses offering engineering opinions are often licensed Professional Engineers (PEs).

Independence and Objectivity

An engineering expert witness is expected to remain independent. Unlike consultants retained to solve problems or design solutions, an expert witness evaluates facts, applies engineering principles, and offers opinions based on evidence.

Credibility depends on:

  • Objectivity
  • Technical rigor
  • Transparency of assumptions
  • Consistency with accepted engineering practice

Typical Engineering Expert Witness Services

Engineering expert witnesses may be asked to perform tasks such as:

  • Reviewing engineering drawings, reports, and calculations
  • Evaluating compliance with applicable standards and codes
  • Analyzing failure modes and causation
  • Assessing reasonableness of engineering decisions
  • Preparing written expert reports
  • Providing sworn testimony or deposition

The scope of work is typically defined in writing and tailored to the needs of the matter.

Forensic Engineering vs. Design Engineering

Forensic engineering focuses on understanding what occurred, why it occurred, and whether professional standards were met.

This differs from design engineering, which involves creating systems or solutions. Forensic work is retrospective and analytical, rather than prescriptive.

Licensing and Qualifications

In Missouri, offering engineering opinions that rely on professional judgment may require licensure as a Professional Engineer. Courts and attorneys frequently seek experts who:

  • Hold active PE licensure
  • Have relevant technical education and experience
  • Can clearly explain technical concepts
  • Are familiar with professional standards and ethics

Licensure alone does not make someone an expert witness, but it is often a baseline requirement.

Reports and Testimony

Expert reports must be well-organized, technically sound, and defensible. Assumptions, methodologies, and limitations should be clearly stated.

During testimony, an engineering expert witness may be asked to:

  • Explain technical findings in plain language
  • Defend methods and conclusions under questioning
  • Clarify what opinions are and are not being offered

Professional demeanor and clarity are as important as technical correctness.

Ethical Considerations

Engineering expert witnesses are bound by professional ethics. Opinions must be honest, unbiased, and based on sufficient facts and analysis. Compensation should not depend on the outcome of a case.

Maintaining ethical standards protects both the integrity of the process and the credibility of the expert.

When an Engineering Expert Witness Is Needed

Common situations include:

  • Construction or design disputes
  • Structural or system failures
  • Professional negligence claims
  • Insurance or property damage matters
  • Regulatory or compliance disputes

Early involvement can help clarify technical issues and narrow disputes.

Closing

Your Permit Was Rejected — Now What? Plan Review Comments and Resubmittals in Missouri

June 15, 2026

Submitting a building permit application is often the first major checkpoint in a Missouri construction project. When plan review returns comments — or rejects the submittal outright — owners and contractors can lose weeks waiting for corrections, especially if the comment letter is long, technical, or spread across multiple disciplines.

A plan review rejection is not always a dead end. In many jurisdictions it is a normal part of the process: reviewers identify missing information, code questions, or design details that must be clarified before a permit can be issued. The path forward usually involves a structured resubmittal that addresses each comment clearly.

This article provides general information about plan review comments and resubmittals in Missouri and when a licensed Professional Engineer (PE) may help prepare or support the technical response.

Plan Review Rejection vs. Stop Work Order vs. Permit Denial

These terms are related but not interchangeable. Understanding the distinction helps determine urgency and next steps:

  • Plan review comments — The application remains in review. The building department has identified deficiencies in the submitted drawings, calculations, or supporting documents. Work generally should not start until a permit is issued, but no active construction is necessarily involved yet.
  • Rejected or incomplete submittal — The package cannot be processed until required items are added. Common examples include missing site plans, unsigned forms, absent trade drawings, or lack of a required engineering seal.
  • Stop work order — Work that already started is halted because of permit, inspection, or field compliance concerns. This is a different stage than a pre-construction plan review hold.
  • Permit denial — Less common for routine projects, but may occur when zoning, use, or fundamental code conflicts cannot be resolved through plan revision alone.

Most owners searching for help after a "permit rejection" are dealing with plan review comments on a first or revised submittal — not a final denial or an active stop order. The resubmittal process described below applies primarily to that situation.

How Plan Review Works in Missouri

Missouri does not have one statewide building plan review office. Municipal building departments, county offices, and regional agencies each administer locally adopted codes and submittal requirements. Although details vary, plan review typically follows a similar pattern:

  1. Application intake and fee payment
  2. Routing to reviewers by discipline (building, structural, mechanical, electrical, plumbing, fire, zoning)
  3. Comment letter or marked-up plans identifying required corrections
  4. Resubmittal addressing each comment
  5. Re-review and permit issuance when approved

Online portals such as Compass KC, SmartGov, and other jurisdiction-specific systems may deliver comments electronically. Whether comments arrive as PDF markups, checklist items, or portal messages, the underlying task is the same: demonstrate that each concern has been addressed.

Common Reasons Plan Review Comments Are Issued

Comment letters can range from a few administrative items to multi-page technical lists. Frequent categories include:

Incomplete or inconsistent submittals

  • Missing site plan, elevations, or construction details
  • Mismatch between architectural and structural drawings
  • Undefined occupancies, construction types, or use groups
  • Applicant or contractor information not filled out correctly

Structural and foundation comments

  • Design outside prescriptive code provisions requiring engineered analysis
  • Missing or inadequate footing, pier, or connection details
  • Beam, joist, or header sizes not shown or not verified
  • Manufacturer package does not match proposed building or site conditions
  • Wind, snow, or seismic design criteria not documented for the site

Mechanical, electrical, and plumbing (MEP) comments

  • HVAC equipment sizing, ventilation, or duct layout not shown
  • Electrical load calculations or service sizing not provided
  • Plumbing fixture counts or drainage details incomplete
  • Fire suppression or alarm coordination missing on commercial projects

Code and life-safety items

  • Egress, guardrail, or stair details not shown
  • Fire separation or rated assembly questions
  • Accessibility-related notes on commercial work
  • Energy code documentation where required

Sealing and professional responsibility

  • Jurisdiction requires a Missouri-licensed PE stamp where submitted plans are unsigned
  • Stamped drawings do not cover the full scope of work shown
  • Calculations referenced on drawings were not included in the submittal

Some comments are straightforward clerical fixes. Others require technical judgment, supplemental calculations, or revised drawings — that is often where engineering involvement becomes appropriate.

How to Read a Plan Review Comment Letter

Before revising anything, organize the comment letter into actionable items:

  • Number each comment and identify which sheet or detail it references
  • Separate administrative items (forms, fees, signatures) from technical items (structural, MEP, fire)
  • Note which discipline reviewer issued each comment when the letter is combined
  • Flag comments that require a design change vs. comments that only require clarification or added notes
  • Identify comments that reference a specific code section — those often need a technical response, not just a drawing note

If any comment is unclear, ask the building department for clarification before resubmitting. Misinterpreting a comment and resubmitting incomplete corrections is a common cause of second and third review cycles.

Preparing a Strong Resubmittal

Reviewers process resubmittals faster when responses are easy to verify. Good practice includes:

  • A comment response letter (or spreadsheet) listing each comment number with a brief description of the correction made
  • Revised drawings with clouded or highlighted changes and a revision date or number
  • New or updated calculations, details, or specifications referenced in the response letter
  • Complete resubmittal package — not only the changed sheets, unless the jurisdiction explicitly accepts partial resubmittals
  • Resubmittal through the same portal or intake method used for the original application

Example response format for a single comment:

Comment 14 — Provide engineered header detail for new opening in bearing wall. Response: Added Detail S-3 on sheet S-2 showing header size, bearing length, and connection. Supporting calculations included as Calculation Package Appendix A. See cloud on sheet A-3 for revised floor plan.

Vague responses such as "addressed on plans" without sheet references or detail numbers often trigger another review cycle.

When a Professional Engineer Can Help

Not every plan review comment requires a PE. Administrative corrections, added notes, or standard prescriptive details may be handled by the designer or contractor. Engineering involvement is commonly appropriate when comments involve:

  • Structural adequacy, load path, or member sizing questions
  • Foundation or connection design not covered by prescriptive tables
  • Required sealed drawings or calculations for the jurisdiction
  • MEP load calculations, equipment sizing, or system coordination
  • Non-standard construction, modified manufacturer packages, or field conditions that differ from original submittals
  • Reviewer requests for an engineering opinion letter explaining an alternate approach

A PE may assist by:

  • Reviewing the comment letter and identifying technical scope
  • Performing calculations or analysis to support revised details
  • Preparing or revising sealed drawings, sketches, or summary letters for resubmittal
  • Drafting technical language for the comment response letter
  • Coordinating with architect, contractor, or trade designers on multi-discipline corrections

Engineering review focused on comment response is often narrower than full project design — scope depends on what was submitted originally and what the reviewer is asking for.

What to Send an Engineer for a Comment-Response Review

To evaluate whether engineering support is needed — and to quote scope — useful intake materials include:

  • Project address and jurisdiction
  • The full plan review comment letter or portal export
  • All drawings and calculations from the original submittal
  • Permit application number and contact at the building department
  • Photos of existing conditions for remodel or addition work
  • Manufacturer documents if a kit or pre-engineered package is involved
  • Target resubmittal deadline if one was given

Providing the complete comment letter upfront avoids scope surprises and helps distinguish quick clarifications from items requiring new analysis.

Resubmittal Timelines, Fees, and Review Cycles

Turnaround after resubmittal varies by jurisdiction, workload, and project complexity. Factors that affect timing include:

  • Whether all comments were addressed in one coordinated package
  • Number of disciplines involved in the review
  • Whether the resubmittal triggers a full re-review or a partial check
  • Resubmittal fees charged by some jurisdictions
  • Completeness of the comment response letter

Multiple review cycles are common on commercial projects and on residential work with structural or MEP complexity. A disciplined first resubmittal — especially on technical comments — reduces total delay even when engineering fees are involved.

Practical Steps After Receiving Plan Review Comments

  1. Confirm whether work is authorized to proceed (usually it is not)
  2. Organize and categorize all comments by discipline and priority
  3. Clarify ambiguous comments with the building department
  4. Assign responsibility for each correction (designer, contractor, engineer, supplier)
  5. Prepare revised documents and a comment response letter before resubmitting
  6. Engage a PE early if comments reference structural, foundation, or sealed-document requirements
  7. Resubmit through the required channel and retain confirmation of receipt

Avoiding Repeat Rejections on Future Projects

Lessons from a difficult plan review often apply to the next project. Helpful habits include:

  • Confirming submittal checklists with the jurisdiction before the first upload
  • Identifying PE sealing requirements early based on project type and local rules
  • Coordinating structural, architectural, and MEP drawings before submission
  • Having engineering review completed before permit submittal on non-prescriptive work
  • Keeping a record of comment themes that recur in a given jurisdiction

Front-end coordination costs less than repeated resubmittals, schedule delay, and contractor standby time.

Closing

A plan review rejection in Missouri is often a request for more complete or code-aligned documentation — not necessarily a fundamental barrier to the project. The fastest path forward is a organized resubmittal that answers each comment specifically, with technical support where structural, MEP, or sealing requirements apply.

When comment letters involve engineering judgment, calculations, or sealed documents, a Professional Engineer may help prepare the response and move the permit back into approval queue.

Fire-Affected Buildings in Missouri: Why an Engineering Review Matters

March 31, 2026

Fires can damage buildings in ways that are not always immediately visible. Even when flames are limited to a portion of a structure, heat, smoke, and water used during firefighting can affect materials and systems throughout the building.

In Missouri, evaluating a fire-affected structure often requires more than a visual walkthrough. An engineering review can help determine the extent of damage, identify safety concerns, and support decisions about repair or continued use.

This article provides general information about fire-affected buildings and when engineering involvement may be appropriate.

How Fire Affects Building Materials

Different materials respond to fire in different ways:

  • Wood may char, lose cross-section, or weaken depending on burn depth
  • Steel can lose strength and stiffness when exposed to high temperatures
  • Concrete may crack or spall due to heat and rapid cooling
  • Masonry can experience cracking or loss of bond
  • Connections and fasteners may be compromised by heat exposure

Some damage is obvious, while other effects may not be visible without closer evaluation.

Hidden Damage After a Fire

Not all fire damage is immediately apparent. Potential hidden issues include:

  • Reduced strength of structural members
  • Heat-affected connections or fasteners
  • Warping or misalignment of framing
  • Compromised floor or roof systems
  • Damage concealed behind finishes

In addition, water from firefighting efforts can introduce moisture-related concerns such as swelling, corrosion, or deterioration over time.

Safety Considerations

Before reoccupying or repairing a fire-affected building, it is important to understand whether the structure remains safe. Concerns may include:

  • Stability of partially damaged framing
  • Risk of progressive collapse in localized areas
  • Reduced load capacity
  • Unsafe access conditions for occupants or workers

An engineering evaluation can help determine whether temporary stabilization or restricted access is needed.

When a Professional Engineer May Be Needed

A Missouri-licensed Professional Engineer may be involved in fire damage situations when:

  • Structural elements may have been exposed to high heat
  • There is visible deformation, cracking, or displacement
  • Portions of the structure require removal or replacement
  • The building department or insurer requires a formal assessment
  • A written report is needed to support repair decisions
  • There is uncertainty about whether the building can be safely occupied

Engineering documentation may help clarify the extent of damage and appropriate next steps.

Engineering Evaluation Process

A fire-related engineering review may include:

  • Site observations of damaged areas
  • Identification of affected structural components
  • Assessment of visible and potential hidden damage
  • Review of original construction if documentation is available
  • Determination of whether components may remain in service
  • Recommendations for further investigation or repair

The scope depends on the building type, severity of fire, and available information.

Coordination with Other Professionals

Fire-damaged buildings are often evaluated by multiple parties, including:

  • Insurance adjusters
  • Contractors and restoration specialists
  • Building officials
  • Environmental or remediation professionals

Engineering input focuses on structural performance and technical evaluation, while other professionals address cleanup, restoration, and regulatory compliance.

Permit and Repair Considerations in Missouri

Repairing fire-damaged structures typically requires permits from the local jurisdiction. In some cases:

  • Engineered drawings may be required for structural repairs
  • Damaged components must be evaluated before replacement
  • Inspectors may require documentation demonstrating safety

Early engineering involvement can help streamline permit approval and reduce delays during reconstruction.

Practical Guidance for Property Owners

After a fire event:

  • Limit access to damaged areas until conditions are understood
  • Document conditions with photographs
  • Avoid removing structural elements without evaluation
  • Coordinate with your insurer and local building department
  • Consider whether engineering review is appropriate before beginning repairs

Early evaluation can help preserve information that may be important for determining cause and repair scope.

Closing

Fire can affect buildings in complex ways that are not always visible at first glance. In Missouri, engineering review of fire-affected structures can help identify safety concerns, evaluate damage, and support informed decisions about repair and reoccupation.

Common Mistakes in Engineering Expert Reports

Why Report Quality Matters

In legal and dispute contexts, engineering expert reports often carry significant weight. Courts, attorneys, insurers, and opposing experts may rely on these reports to understand technical issues and evaluate causation. The quality of an expert report can influence not only how persuasive the analysis is, but whether it is taken seriously at all.

Even technically correct opinions can lose credibility if they are poorly presented or inadequately supported.

Lack of Clear Scope and Questions

A common issue in expert reports is the absence of a clearly defined scope. Effective reports state:

  • The specific questions the expert was asked to address
  • The boundaries of the analysis
  • What issues are outside the scope of the report

Without this clarity, readers may misinterpret conclusions or assume opinions extend beyond what was actually analyzed.

Unstated or Unsupported Assumptions

All engineering analysis involves assumptions. Problems arise when assumptions are:

  • Not disclosed
  • Not justified
  • Inconsistent with available evidence

Courts and opposing experts often focus on assumptions. Failure to state and support them can undermine otherwise sound analysis.

Insufficient Connection to Evidence

Expert opinions must be grounded in the facts of the case. Reports that present general engineering theory without tying it to specific evidence are often less persuasive.

Effective reports:

  • Cite relevant documents, data, and observations
  • Explain how evidence supports conclusions
  • Distinguish between observed facts and inferred conditions

Failure to Address Alternative Explanations

Credible engineering analysis considers plausible alternative causes. Reports that present a single explanation without addressing reasonable alternatives may appear incomplete or biased.

Addressing alternative explanations does not weaken a report; it often strengthens it by demonstrating that competing hypotheses were considered and evaluated.

Overstating Certainty

Engineering analysis often involves uncertainty. Overstating confidence or presenting conclusions as absolute can be misleading.

Responsible reports:

  • Acknowledge data limitations
  • Identify uncertainties or assumptions
  • Avoid definitive language when evidence does not support it

Transparency about uncertainty enhances credibility.

Blurring Technical and Legal Conclusions

Engineering experts provide technical opinions, not legal judgments. Reports that drift into legal conclusions—such as fault, negligence, or liability—risk being discounted or challenged.

Maintaining a clear distinction between technical causation and legal responsibility helps preserve the integrity of the report.

Poor Organization and Communication

Even strong technical analysis can be undermined by poor structure or unclear writing. Common communication issues include:

  • Dense, jargon-heavy language
  • Unclear presentation of methods
  • Disorganized findings
  • Lack of summaries or conclusions

Clear organization and plain language help non-technical readers understand the analysis and reasoning.

Inadequate Documentation of Methods

Courts and opposing experts often scrutinize methodology. Reports should describe:

  • The methods used
  • Why those methods are appropriate
  • Any limitations of the methods

Without this documentation, opinions may appear unsupported.

Closing

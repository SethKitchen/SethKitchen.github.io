How Courts Evaluate Engineering Causation Testimony

The Role of Engineering Testimony in Legal Proceedings

In cases involving technical or engineering issues, courts often rely on expert testimony to understand causation. Engineering causation testimony helps explain whether and how a system failed, what factors contributed to that failure, and whether proposed explanations are technically sound.

Courts do not evaluate engineering testimony based on conclusions alone. They examine the methods, reasoning, and reliability underlying the opinions offered.

Admissibility vs. Persuasiveness

Courts typically distinguish between two related but separate questions:

  • Admissibility: Whether the testimony meets legal standards to be considered
  • Persuasiveness: How much weight the court gives the testimony

An opinion may be admissible but still unpersuasive if it lacks clarity, support, or credibility.

Methodology and Reliability

Courts evaluate whether an engineer’s analysis is grounded in reliable methodology. Factors commonly considered include:

  • Use of accepted engineering principles
  • Consistency with professional standards or practices
  • Application of methods appropriate to the problem
  • Logical connection between evidence and conclusions

Courts are generally skeptical of opinions that rely on unexplained assumptions or unsupported leaps in reasoning.

Connection to the Evidence

Engineering causation testimony must be tied to the facts of the case. Courts examine whether:

  • The engineer reviewed relevant documents and data
  • Physical evidence supports the proposed causation
  • Alternative explanations were considered and addressed
  • The timing and sequence of events are consistent

Opinions that are abstract or disconnected from case-specific evidence are often given less weight.

Transparency of Assumptions

All engineering analysis involves assumptions. Courts do not expect assumptions to be eliminated, but they do expect them to be:

  • Identified explicitly
  • Reasonable in context
  • Consistently applied

Unstated or shifting assumptions can undermine credibility, even if the technical analysis is otherwise sound.

Qualifications and Scope

Courts consider whether the expert’s qualifications align with the opinions offered. Relevant factors may include:

  • Licensure and professional standing
  • Education and technical experience
  • Familiarity with the subject matter
  • Experience with similar systems or failures

Courts may discount testimony that extends beyond the expert’s demonstrated area of competence.

Treatment of Alternative Causes

Credible engineering testimony addresses alternative causation theories. Courts look for evidence that the expert:

  • Considered other plausible explanations
  • Evaluated them using engineering principles
  • Explained why they were accepted or rejected

Failure to engage with alternative causes can suggest incomplete or biased analysis.

Communication and Clarity

Even technically correct opinions may be ineffective if they are poorly communicated. Courts value testimony that:

  • Clearly separates facts, analysis, and conclusions
  • Uses plain language where possible
  • Avoids unnecessary jargon
  • Acknowledges uncertainty when appropriate

Clear explanations help courts understand both the reasoning and its limitations.

Independence and Professional Conduct

Courts are attentive to signs of bias or advocacy. Factors that may affect credibility include:

  • Consistency of opinions across cases
  • Compensation arrangements
  • Willingness to acknowledge unfavorable facts
  • Adherence to professional ethics

Engineering experts are expected to assist the court, not argue a case.

Limits of Engineering Testimony

Engineering experts provide technical opinions, not legal conclusions. Courts evaluate causation testimony within the broader legal framework, which may involve additional considerations beyond engineering analysis.

Responsible experts respect these boundaries.

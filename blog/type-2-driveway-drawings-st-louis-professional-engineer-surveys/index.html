Type 2 Driveway Drawings in St. Louis: How a Professional Engineer Can Help Using Survey Data

May 17, 2026

When a property owner or developer in St. Louis, Missouri plans a new driveway approach or modifies an existing one, local jurisdictions and state transportation agencies often classify that driveway by type. Different driveway types carry different design requirements, and preparing the drawings to meet those requirements is where engineering and survey work intersect.

This article provides general information about driveway drawing classifications—including Type 2—and how a licensed Professional Engineer (PE) can assist in preparing documentation that reflects site conditions accurately.

What Are Driveway Drawing Types?

Municipalities, counties, and state transportation agencies commonly classify driveways based on factors such as:

  • Expected traffic volume and vehicle types
  • Whether the property is residential or commercial
  • The functional classification of the adjacent roadway
  • Width, turning radius, and geometric requirements

In the St. Louis area, driveway approach permits—whether issued by a city, county, or MoDOT for state-route-adjacent properties—often reference specific design standards tied to these classifications.

Common Driveway Classifications

While specific definitions vary by jurisdiction, driveway types are generally organized along these lines.

Type 1 — Residential or Low-Volume

Typically applies to single-family or low-traffic residential driveways. Requirements often include:

  • Minimum and maximum width limits
  • Required setbacks from intersections or fire hydrants
  • Curb cut geometry or apron specifications
  • Drainage provisions at the right-of-way line

Many Type 1 applications can be handled with simpler plan submissions, though site conditions can still require more detailed documentation.

Type 2 — Commercial or Moderate-Volume

A Type 2 driveway designation commonly applies to commercial or higher-volume access points. These driveways are subject to stricter geometric and safety requirements, such as:

  • Larger minimum widths and turning radii
  • Sight distance requirements relative to roadway speed limits
  • Pavement taper or deceleration lane considerations on higher-volume roads
  • Potential requirements for traffic impact documentation
  • Storm drainage and curb design at the access point

In St. Louis, commercial driveway approaches along arterials and collector streets frequently fall into this category. Drawing packages for Type 2 driveways often require detailed plan and profile views grounded in actual survey data.

Type 3 and Higher — High-Volume or Complex Access

Higher driveway type classifications typically apply to high-traffic generators such as shopping centers, fuel stations, or industrial facilities. These may involve:

  • Full traffic engineering analysis
  • Dedicated turn lanes or signal coordination
  • Multiple access points and internal circulation review
  • Coordination with MoDOT or St. Louis County/City transportation staff

Engineering involvement in these applications typically extends beyond drafting to include calculations, traffic studies, and agency coordination.

Why Survey Data Matters for Driveway Drawings

Driveway drawings that will be submitted for permit review must accurately reflect existing conditions. Survey data provides the factual foundation for that documentation. Relevant survey information may include:

  • Property lines and right-of-way limits, which define where the driveway approach begins and ends
  • Existing grades and elevations along the edge of roadway and across the proposed driveway area
  • Locations of existing curbs, gutters, sidewalks, and utilities within or near the right-of-way
  • Sight distance conditions based on topography and adjacent features
  • Setbacks from adjacent driveways, intersections, and structures

Without survey data, driveway drawings may not reflect the actual site accurately enough to satisfy plan reviewers or identify potential drainage and geometric issues before construction begins.

How a Professional Engineer Can Help

A licensed PE in Missouri may assist with driveway drawing preparation in several ways:

Interpreting Survey Information

Survey data often requires interpretation before it can be used effectively in a drawing set. A PE can review existing topographic or boundary surveys, identify relevant control points, and translate field conditions into engineering documentation.

Preparing Plan and Profile Drawings

Driveway drawings submitted to St. Louis jurisdictions typically require plan views showing the proposed geometry and profile views showing how grades transition from the roadway to the property. A PE can prepare these drawings to applicable standards and mark them appropriately for submission.

Addressing Drainage at the Driveway Approach

One of the more technically sensitive aspects of driveway design is drainage. Grading must prevent water from being directed into the roadway or back toward adjacent structures. Where grades are constrained, engineering calculations may be needed to document that the proposed design handles runoff adequately.

Supporting the Permit Application

In St. Louis, permit applications for Type 2 or higher driveways may require drawings prepared or reviewed by a licensed engineer. A PE can sign and seal documents where required, respond to reviewer comments, and help coordinate technical issues that arise during the review process.

Coordinating Engineering and Survey Work

Engineering and survey work serve distinct but complementary roles in driveway drawing preparation:

  • A licensed land surveyor establishes property lines, locates existing features, and provides accurate field measurements
  • A Professional Engineer uses that survey data to design the geometric layout, evaluate grades and drainage, and prepare drawings that comply with applicable standards

In some projects, the same firm provides both services. In others, a property owner or developer engages separate professionals. Either way, early coordination between surveying and engineering reduces the risk of rework and helps drawings reach permit-ready status more efficiently.

St. Louis-Area Permit Context

The St. Louis region includes the City of St. Louis, St. Louis County, and numerous municipalities, each of which may have its own driveway permit process and design standards. Some considerations specific to the area include:

  • Driveways accessing MoDOT-maintained routes (such as certain state highways and lettered roads) require MoDOT access permits, which have their own drawing and review requirements
  • St. Louis County Public Works and individual municipalities within the county may have separate permit processes for driveways on county or city roads
  • Urban areas within the City of St. Louis often involve additional review considerations related to curb geometry, existing streetscape features, and stormwater management

Confirming which agency has jurisdiction over the adjacent roadway is an important first step before preparing driveway drawings.

When Engineering Involvement May Be Appropriate

Not every driveway project requires a Professional Engineer. But engineering involvement may be appropriate when:

  • The jurisdiction or reviewing agency requires engineered drawings
  • The driveway is classified as Type 2 or higher based on property use or roadway type
  • Grading or drainage conditions present design challenges not addressed by standard details
  • The proposed access point involves sight distance constraints or geometric complications
  • The project is part of a larger site development requiring coordinated engineering documentation
  • The property owner wants an independent technical review of proposed driveway plans

Closing

Driveway drawings in St. Louis, Missouri—particularly for Type 2 and higher classifications—require accurate site data and careful attention to geometric, drainage, and permitting requirements. A Professional Engineer can help translate survey information into compliant, permit-ready documentation and work through technical issues that arise during the review process.

Small Residential & Light Commercial Structural Reviews in Missouri: What You Get, What I Need, and What It Costs

February 10, 2026

What you get, what I need, and what it costs.

When a home or small building project involves a new opening, a removed wall, a sagging beam, or a permit request, the next step is often the same: a clear structural review with calculations and a work product that a contractor and permitting office can understand.

This post explains what a small residential/light commercial structural review typically includes, when a desktop review is appropriate, when a site visit is recommended, and how to request a quote efficiently.

Common projects that trigger a structural review

Most requests I receive fall into a few categories:

  • New openings: garage doors, widened doorways, pass-throughs, egress windows
  • Load-bearing wall changes: "Is this wall structural?" and "What header/beam do I need?"
  • Beam/post/footing checks: new point loads, posts on slabs, basement beam replacements
  • Decks and porches: ledger connections, posts/footings, beams, lateral resistance
  • Roof framing concerns: rafter sizing, ridge/ledger conditions, deflection/sag questions
  • Permit support: when a city/county asks for engineer calculations or an engineer letter

If you're unsure whether your project is structural, that's normal. A quick intake (photos + a few measurements) is usually enough to determine whether engineering is needed and what scope makes sense.

Desktop review vs. site visit

Many small projects can start as a desktop structural review, meaning the analysis is performed using client-provided information (photos, measurements, and any existing drawings). Desktop review can be a cost-effective way to get answers quickly when conditions are well documented.

A site visit may be recommended or required when:

  • Framing conditions are concealed or unclear
  • Measurements can't be reliably confirmed
  • The load path depends on details not visible in photos
  • The jurisdiction requires observation, verification, or specific documentation
  • The risk profile is higher (unusual geometry, significant alteration, signs of distress)

The goal is not to upsell a site visit. The goal is to make sure the engineering has a sufficient basis and the work product is defensible.

What you typically receive

Deliverables depend on the project and the jurisdiction, but commonly include:

  • Written summary letter or memo describing scope, assumptions, and findings
  • Calculations documenting loads, checks, and governing assumptions
  • Sketches or marked-up plans sufficient to communicate engineering intent
  • Letter/report or calculations for permit submittal (sealed when appropriate, and when scope and information support responsible charge)

If the city/county needs a specific format (certain notes, plan sheets, or code references), I'll align the deliverable to that requirement where practical.

What I need from you for a fast quote

The fastest way to get an accurate scope and fee is to send:

  • Project address and jurisdiction (city/county) if known
  • A short description of what's changing and why (one paragraph is fine)
  • Photos: wide shots + close-ups (include context like adjacent walls/ceilings)
  • Basic measurements, such as:
    • Spans, spacing, member sizes
    • Wall thickness and framing direction
    • Beam/post locations
    • Opening width, bearing lengths
  • Any existing drawings (even contractor sketches can help)
  • Any permit comments or written requirements you were given

If measurements or field conditions change later, the analysis may need revision, so good intake information saves time and money.

Typical pricing for small structural reviews

Every project is different, but to help set expectations:

  • Typical desktop structural review (client-provided measurements/photos): $500-$1,500 depending on complexity
  • Letter/report or calculations for permit submittal (sealed when appropriate): $250-$500
  • Site visits: quoted separately based on scope, travel, and documentation needs

Final scope and fee are confirmed after a brief intake so expectations are clear on both sides.

What a structural review is (and isn't)

A professional engineering review is not a promise of permit approval, and it isn't a rubber stamp. It's a documented engineering work product based on the information available, appropriate assumptions, and applicable requirements.

If the actual field conditions differ from what's provided, conclusions may change. When needed, the scope should include the right level of verification (which may mean a site visit).

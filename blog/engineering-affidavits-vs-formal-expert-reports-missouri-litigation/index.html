Engineering Affidavits vs. Formal Expert Reports in Missouri Litigation

July 2, 2026

Attorneys handling construction defects, insurance disputes, product failures, and other technical matters in Missouri often need engineering input in written form long before trial. Two common deliverables are engineering affidavits and formal expert reports — but they are not interchangeable.

The appropriate format depends on procedural stage, forum, discovery obligations, and how the technical opinion will be used. Confusing the two can lead to underdeveloped opinions, discovery disputes, or documents that do not support the intended legal purpose.

This article provides general information about engineering affidavits and formal expert reports in Missouri litigation and how a licensed Professional Engineer (PE) may approach each.

What Is an Engineering Affidavit?

In litigation contexts, an affidavit is typically a sworn written statement made under oath. An engineering affidavit may summarize a PE's observations, opinions, or factual attestations related to a technical issue.

Engineering affidavits are often used for purposes such as:

  • Supporting motions (for example, summary judgment or preliminary relief)
  • Attesting to site conditions, measurements, or observations
  • Providing a concise technical statement on a narrow issue
  • Supporting procedural filings where a full expert report is not yet required
  • Documenting an engineer's qualifications or limited opinion on a discrete question

Affidavits are generally shorter and more focused than formal expert reports. They may not include the full methodology, literature review, or detailed analysis expected in a comprehensive expert disclosure.

What Is a Formal Expert Report?

A formal expert report is a structured technical document prepared for litigation or dispute proceedings. In Missouri court practice, expert reports are commonly exchanged during discovery and may be used at deposition, trial, or in connection with Daubert or similar admissibility challenges.

A formal engineering expert report typically includes:

  • Defined scope and questions presented
  • Expert qualifications and basis for opinions
  • Documents, data, and evidence reviewed
  • Methodology and engineering analysis
  • Stated assumptions and limitations
  • Opinions and conclusions responsive to the assignment

The level of detail is usually substantially greater than an affidavit because opposing parties need sufficient information to understand, test, and respond to the expert's opinions.

These are general patterns. Specific case rules, court orders, and counsel strategy determine what format is appropriate in a given matter.

When Each Format May Be Used in Missouri Litigation

Affidavits — Common Situations

  • Early case stages before full expert discovery is complete
  • Narrow technical questions that do not require extensive analysis
  • Motion practice where a concise sworn statement is sufficient
  • Attesting to observations made during a site visit
  • Supporting procedural deadlines when a full report is still in development

Formal Expert Reports — Common Situations

  • Expert witness disclosure under Missouri discovery rules
  • Complex causation, failure analysis, or design-standard questions
  • Cases proceeding to deposition and trial testimony
  • Matters where opposing counsel needs full methodology to prepare cross-examination
  • Disputes where admissibility of expert testimony may be challenged

Relationship to Mediation and Arbitration

Not every technical dispute proceeds to trial. In mediation and arbitration, parties may use engineering opinions in forms that differ from court-mandated expert disclosures:

  • A focused technical memo or letter may be sufficient for settlement discussions
  • An affidavit-style summary may support a position without the cost of a full report
  • Arbitration rules may require expert reports with less or different detail than Missouri court rules
  • Engineering support before trial may help parties narrow issues and avoid litigation altogether

The forum and stage of the dispute should guide format selection. Engineering input useful in mediation may still need to be expanded into a formal report if the matter later proceeds to litigation.

Admissibility and Sufficiency Considerations

Courts evaluating engineering opinions — whether in affidavit or report form — may consider factors such as:

  • Whether the expert is qualified in the relevant discipline
  • Whether the opinion is based on sufficient facts or data
  • Whether reliable principles and methods were applied
  • Whether the opinion is tied to the facts of the case and stated assumptions

A short affidavit may be adequate for a limited purpose but insufficient to support broad expert opinions at trial. Conversely, a lengthy report may be unnecessary for a discrete procedural filing. Attorneys and engineers should align format with the intended use.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating an affidavit as a substitute for a full expert report when discovery rules require more detail
  • Stating broad conclusions in an affidavit without supporting methodology
  • Preparing a formal report that does not clearly define scope or assumptions
  • Failing to update opinions when new information becomes available
  • Using litigation documents prepared for one forum without adjusting for another (court vs. arbitration vs. mediation)

How a Professional Engineer May Approach Each Assignment

A Missouri PE providing litigation support typically:

  • Confirms the forum, procedural stage, and intended use of the document with retaining counsel
  • Defines scope narrowly for affidavits and comprehensively for expert reports
  • States assumptions, limitations, and the basis for opinions clearly
  • Maintains objectivity and documents the information relied upon
  • Scopes fee and timeline according to the depth of analysis required

Engineers do not provide legal advice about which document format satisfies court rules. That determination rests with counsel based on the specific case and procedural posture.

Practical Guidance for Attorneys and Clients

  • Identify the forum and procedural deadline before engaging an engineer
  • Specify whether an affidavit, technical memo, or full expert report is needed
  • Provide clear questions and relevant documents early
  • Consider whether early technical input in mediation may reduce later litigation expense
  • Plan for the possibility that an affidavit-level opinion may need expansion if the case proceeds

Closing

Engineering affidavits and formal expert reports serve different roles in Missouri litigation. Affidavits provide sworn, focused statements for procedural and narrow technical purposes; formal expert reports provide the detailed analysis, methodology, and documentation expected in discovery and trial settings. Selecting the right format — and the right scope of engineering support — helps align technical work with legal strategy.

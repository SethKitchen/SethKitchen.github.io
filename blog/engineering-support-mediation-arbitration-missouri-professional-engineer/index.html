Engineering Support for Mediation and Arbitration in Missouri: When a Professional Engineer Can Help Before Trial

June 18, 2026

Many engineering-related disputes in Missouri never reach a courtroom. Construction disagreements, insurance claims, contractor performance questions, and system failure matters are often addressed through mediation or arbitration long before trial is scheduled.

Technical questions still matter in those settings. Parties need to understand what failed, what standards apply, what repairs are reasonable, and how strongly each side's engineering position is supported. A licensed Professional Engineer (PE) can provide that clarity — but the scope, format, and timing of engineering support often differ from a full expert witness engagement prepared for trial.

This article provides general information about engineering support for mediation and arbitration in Missouri and when PE involvement may be appropriate before a case proceeds to litigation.

Mediation, Arbitration, and Trial: Different Forums, Different Engineering Needs

Attorneys and property owners sometimes search for an "engineering expert for mediation" because the forum shapes what kind of technical help is useful. At a high level:

  • Mediation — A facilitated negotiation. Engineering input is often used to clarify facts, narrow disagreements, and help parties evaluate settlement ranges. Formal testimony may not occur at all.
  • Arbitration — A private adjudication process, usually more structured than mediation. Engineering opinions may be submitted in writing, presented at a hearing, and subjected to cross-examination depending on the rules and scope of the proceeding.
  • Trial — Court litigation with formal rules of evidence, discovery, depositions, and jury or bench trial procedures. Expert witness work is typically more extensive and procedural.

Engineering support for mediation or arbitration can be narrower, faster, and more settlement-oriented than trial-prepared expert work — while still requiring the same professional independence and technical rigor.

Why Engineering Matters Before Trial

Technical disputes rarely turn on a single document. Parties may disagree about causation, code compliance, design adequacy, repair scope, or the reasonableness of costs. Without engineering analysis, mediation and arbitration sessions can stall on unresolved technical questions.

Engineering support can help by:

  • Translating complex system or structural issues into clear findings
  • Identifying what is supported by evidence vs. what remains uncertain
  • Comparing opposing technical positions and explaining key differences
  • Defining realistic repair or remediation scopes for settlement discussions
  • Helping counsel and clients assess the strength of technical claims before committing to trial costs

Early technical clarity can reduce the duration and expense of dispute resolution even when the parties do not fully agree.

Common Disputes Where Engineering Support Is Requested

Mediation and arbitration engineering support appears across many project types, including:

  • Construction defect and workmanship disagreements (structural, MEP, envelope)
  • Contractor–owner disputes over design responsibility
  • Insurance claims involving building or equipment damage
  • Delay and performance disputes tied to system failures
  • Professional liability questions involving engineering or design documents
  • Property condition disagreements in commercial transactions
  • Warranty and callback disputes on mechanical, electrical, or control systems

The appropriate engineering scope depends on the dispute, available documents, and whether the forum expects a written report, a presentation, live testimony, or a consultative opinion for internal strategy.

Engineering Support in Mediation

In mediation, a Professional Engineer may work behind the scenes, participate in caucus preparation, or — when agreed by the parties — assist in joint technical discussions. Typical roles include:

  • Reviewing opposing expert reports or contractor claims and summarizing strengths and weaknesses
  • Preparing a technical memo or settlement-oriented engineering opinion for counsel
  • Helping a client understand repair alternatives and relative costs
  • Identifying areas of technical agreement that can be removed from negotiation
  • Attending mediation to explain findings in plain language when requested and agreed

Mediation-focused engineering work often emphasizes:

  • Clarity over exhaustive litigation-style documentation
  • Practical repair or scope options
  • Explicit assumptions and limitations
  • Findings that help parties evaluate risk, not only assign blame

Mediation engineering is not advocacy, but it is often structured to support informed settlement decisions rather than courtroom persuasion alone.

Engineering Support in Arbitration

Arbitration is typically more formal than mediation. Engineering involvement may resemble expert witness work in some respects, but scope and procedure depend on the arbitration agreement, governing rules, and the arbitrator's directions.

A PE may assist in arbitration by:

  • Preparing a written expert report or technical opinion for submission
  • Responding to opposing expert opinions or rebuttal reports
  • Explaining failure modes, causation, or code-related questions
  • Testifying at an arbitration hearing when required
  • Reviewing documentary evidence, inspection records, and test data

Even in arbitration, engineering support may remain narrower than a multi-year trial preparation path — especially when the dispute is limited to a defined technical issue such as a roof assembly, foundation distress, HVAC performance, or a single system failure.

How This Differs From Trial Expert Witness Work

Trial expert witness engagements often include extensive discovery, depositions, Daubert or admissibility issues, jury-oriented communication, and long-form report development tied to court schedules. Mediation and arbitration support may involve some of the same analytical work but frequently differs in:

  • Timing — Engagements may be shorter and tied to a mediation date or arbitration hearing window
  • Deliverables — Technical memos, summary opinions, or focused reports rather than full litigation packages
  • Audience — Mediators, arbitrators, and negotiating parties rather than juries
  • Purpose — Clarifying technical tradeoffs and settlement options, not only preparing for cross-examination
  • Scope — Sometimes limited to reviewing existing reports or answering discrete technical questions

A matter that begins with mediation or arbitration support may later require expanded expert witness services if settlement fails. Scope should be defined clearly at the outset so expectations align with the forum and timeline.

Types of Engineering Deliverables

Depending on forum and counsel strategy, engineering support may take several forms:

Consultative technical review

Internal analysis for attorney or client use, without a formal report intended for the opposing party. Useful for early case assessment before mediation.

Technical memo or summary opinion

A written document explaining scope, findings, assumptions, and limitations in a format suited to dispute resolution rather than full trial discovery.

Expert report for arbitration

A more formal written opinion submitted under arbitration rules, potentially with rebuttal or supplemental reporting.

Opposing expert critique

Independent review of another engineer's report, identifying methodological issues, unsupported assumptions, or gaps in the analysis.

Live presentation or hearing testimony

Verbal explanation of findings at mediation, arbitration, or technical settlement conferences when requested and within scope.

Independence and Professional Responsibilities

Whether the work supports mediation, arbitration, or trial, a Professional Engineer must maintain independence and base opinions on sufficient technical analysis. Engineering conclusions should not be shaped to fit a preferred settlement outcome.

Credible engineering support in dispute resolution typically includes:

  • Clear definition of scope and materials reviewed
  • Transparent assumptions and limitations
  • Distinction between observed facts and professional opinions
  • Methodology that can be explained to non-engineers and technical reviewers alike
  • Acknowledgment when available evidence is incomplete or conflicting

Objectivity strengthens the value of engineering input in settlement discussions because parties can rely on the analysis even when they disagree on liability or cost allocation.

When to Engage an Engineer

Timing affects cost and usefulness. Common engagement points include:

  • Before mediation is scheduled — To evaluate whether a technical position is well supported and to identify realistic settlement parameters
  • After receiving an opposing expert report — To assess strengths, weaknesses, and response strategy
  • Shortly before mediation — To prepare counsel and client with concise technical summaries
  • At arbitration case management — To define report deadlines, hearing scope, and technical issues in dispute

Waiting until the eve of a session can limit site visits, testing, or supplemental analysis. Early engagement often produces more useful settlement information.

What to Provide for an Initial Engineering Review

Useful intake materials for mediation or arbitration support often include:

  • Brief description of the dispute and desired forum
  • Relevant contracts, plans, specifications, and change orders
  • Inspection reports, photos, and maintenance records
  • Opposing party or expert reports, if available
  • Insurance correspondence or claim documentation
  • Mediation statement, arbitration demand, or technical issue list
  • Key dates (mediation, hearing, or report deadline)

A focused document set helps distinguish a quick technical review from a broader forensic investigation requiring additional scope.

Practical Considerations for Attorneys and Property Owners

  • Define whether the engineer is retained as a consulting expert, testifying expert, or reviewer of existing work
  • Confirm whether written work product may be discoverable in later litigation if settlement fails
  • Align report format and depth with mediation or arbitration rules and schedules
  • Identify discrete technical questions rather than asking for open-ended opinions on entire projects when possible
  • Consider whether joint expert meetings or neutral evaluation may resolve narrow technical issues efficiently

These decisions are legal and strategic as well as technical; engineering scope should be coordinated with counsel.

Closing

Mediation and arbitration are common paths for resolving engineering-related disputes in Missouri. Technical clarity remains essential in those forums — often with different timing, format, and purpose than trial expert witness work.

A Professional Engineer can support settlement-focused dispute resolution through independent review, focused reports, and clear communication of technical findings. Early engagement may help parties evaluate options before litigation costs escalate.

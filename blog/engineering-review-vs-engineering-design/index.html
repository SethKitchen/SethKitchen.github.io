Engineering Review vs. Engineering Design: Understanding the Difference

February 11, 2026

Two Distinct but Related Professional Services

Engineering design and engineering review are closely related activities, but they serve different purposes and carry different responsibilities. Understanding the distinction is important for project owners, attorneys, and decision-makers who rely on engineering judgment.

In Missouri, both activities may constitute the practice of engineering when professional judgment is applied.

What Is Engineering Design?

Engineering design involves creating technical solutions to meet defined objectives or constraints. Design work may include:

  • Selecting system configurations
  • Determining performance criteria
  • Establishing specifications and parameters
  • Preparing drawings or technical documentation
  • Evaluating alternatives and trade-offs

Design is forward-looking and solution-oriented. It requires assumptions about operating conditions, loads, interactions, and performance expectations.

What Is Engineering Review?

Engineering review evaluates work prepared by others or examines an existing system. Review work may include:

  • Assessing technical adequacy of drawings or calculations
  • Evaluating system performance against standards
  • Reviewing assumptions and methodologies
  • Identifying inconsistencies or risk areas
  • Providing documented engineering opinions

Review is analytical rather than creative. It focuses on whether the work or system meets reasonable engineering expectations.

Differences in Responsibility

Both design and review involve professional responsibility, but the scope differs.

Design responsibility centers on creating a system or solution that performs as intended.

Review responsibility centers on evaluating whether a system or work product is reasonable, compliant, or technically sound.

When a Professional Engineer seals a document, they assume responsibility consistent with the scope of their involvement, whether design or review.

Review Does Not Mean Approval

Engineering review should not be confused with approval authority. A reviewing engineer may identify deficiencies, confirm adequacy, or recommend further investigation, but regulatory approval is typically the responsibility of permitting authorities or governing bodies.

Clear communication of scope helps prevent misunderstandings about what a review does and does not represent.

Forensic and Litigation Contexts

In forensic engineering and expert witness contexts, review is often retrospective. Engineers may be asked to:

  • Evaluate prior design decisions
  • Assess whether accepted practices were followed
  • Determine whether assumptions were reasonable
  • Analyze causation in system failures

In these situations, the engineer is not redesigning the system but analyzing its past performance.

Limitations and Boundaries

Engineering review is limited by:

  • Available documentation
  • Access to system components
  • Time and scope constraints
  • Assumptions required due to incomplete data

Responsible engineering reports identify these limitations clearly.

Why the Distinction Matters

Confusion between design and review can lead to unrealistic expectations or misplaced responsibility. Clarifying whether a service involves original design or independent evaluation helps align expectations and protect all parties involved.

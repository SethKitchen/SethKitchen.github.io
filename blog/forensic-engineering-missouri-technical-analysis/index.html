Forensic Engineering in Missouri: Technical Analysis After a Failure

Understanding forensic engineering practice in Missouri: technical investigation of failures, incidents, and disputes by a licensed Professional Engineer.

What Forensic Engineering Is — and Is Not

Forensic engineering focuses on the investigation of engineering-related failures, incidents, or disputes. The purpose is not advocacy, but technical understanding: determining what occurred, how systems behaved, and whether engineering practices were consistent with accepted standards.

In Missouri, forensic engineering opinions offered by a licensed Professional Engineer must be based on sufficient facts, appropriate analysis, and the engineer's area of competence.

This article outlines how forensic engineering is typically applied and when it may be appropriate to engage a Missouri-licensed Professional Engineer.

Common Situations Involving Forensic Engineering

Forensic engineering may be involved in a wide range of situations, including:

  • System or equipment failures
  • Software-controlled system malfunctions
  • Industrial or infrastructure incidents
  • Construction or design disputes
  • Product performance issues
  • Insurance or liability investigations

In many cases, forensic analysis occurs months or years after the original work was performed, requiring careful reconstruction of events from available evidence.

The Forensic Engineering Process

While each investigation is unique, forensic engineering often follows a structured process:

Evidence Collection

Review of drawings, specifications, logs, test results, photographs, and physical evidence.

System Understanding

Analysis of how the system was intended to function, including control logic, operating conditions, and interfaces.

Failure Analysis

Identification of failure modes, contributing factors, and sequence of events.

Engineering Evaluation

Assessment of whether engineering decisions aligned with accepted practices at the time the work was performed.

Documentation

Preparation of clear, technically grounded findings and opinions.

The emphasis is on reproducible analysis and defensible reasoning rather than conclusions drawn from limited data.

Software and Complex Systems in Forensic Analysis

Modern forensic engineering increasingly involves software-driven and distributed systems. In these cases, failures may arise from timing, state transitions, data dependencies, or unexpected interactions between components.

Forensic analysis of such systems may include:

  • Review of source code or configuration artifacts
  • Analysis of logs, telemetry, or audit trails
  • Evaluation of control strategies and fault handling
  • Reconstruction of system state over time

Understanding both software behavior and physical system constraints is often necessary to form a reliable engineering opinion.

Expert Engineering Opinions and Legal Contexts

Forensic engineering findings are frequently used in legal, insurance, or regulatory contexts. When acting as an expert, a Professional Engineer's role is to provide objective technical opinions — not legal conclusions.

Key principles include:

  • Remaining within the engineer's area of expertise
  • Avoiding speculation beyond available evidence
  • Clearly stating assumptions and limitations
  • Distinguishing facts from professional judgment

Professional engineers are ethically obligated to present opinions honestly and without bias.

When to Engage a Forensic Engineer

Engaging a forensic engineer may be appropriate when:

  • The cause of a failure is unclear or disputed
  • Technical evidence requires expert interpretation
  • An independent engineering opinion is needed
  • Engineering documentation or practices are questioned

Early involvement can help preserve evidence and reduce uncertainty as investigations progress.

Closing

This article provides general information about forensic engineering practice in Missouri. It does not constitute engineering advice or establish a professional engagement.

Forensic engineering requires careful analysis, technical rigor, and professional judgment grounded in engineering principles and experience.
